A Pathological Example

Suppose I give every person in this room an integer number and inquire whether the sum of the numbers is odd or even, while constraining that each person may only convey one bit of information

Clearly this problem may be solved by picking a basis (even = 0, odd = 1), adding the bit that is conveyed to you to your number and passing the appropriate bit to the next person

A Pathological Example

Suppose now I give you a non-integer, positive number and ask you to do the same thing, but using a qubit rather than a bit, I strictly specify that the sum of all the numbers in the room is an integer. (You may assume that everyone has a pre-agreed basis)

Again we can use the basis that even = |0>, odd = |1> and store the non-integer component in the phase |\psi> = a|0> + b|1>. When given someone else's qubit you add it to yours without measuring it, then pass the new state to the next person. The final person performs the measurement and has the result.

Quantum Algorithms

Deutch-Jozsa: Free$^{*}$ parallelism

$^{*}$:Where "Free" indicates an exponential speedup if you want to solve a particular single problem, if you want other problems solved you will have to find your own algorithm

Grover's Algorithm: Search an unsorted list for an element$^{**}$ in $O(\sqrt(n))$, best classical is $O(n)$

$^{**}$: Assuming that you know the number of elements in the list that correspond to the element you are searching for, otherwise this algorithm will not work effectively

Shor's Algorithm: Because fourier transforms are unitary, and nobody really needed asymmetric cryptography anyway

Shor's Algorithm

Solves the prime factor problem

The classical approach: GNFS

Generate polynomials and hope that their roots form a smooth ring such that the 'square root' of the ring is a homomorphism to the prime factors

Shor's algorithm: Find the period of the prime field using the quantum fourier transform, the 'square root' then gives the prime factors
